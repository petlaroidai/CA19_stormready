1. Executive Summary
The Problem
California's 19th Congressional District (Santa Cruz, Monterey, and the Santa Cruz Mountains) faces a unique convergence of natural disasters: atmospheric rivers that flood coastal towns and close Hwy 1, wildfires that burn through mountain communities (the CZU Lightning Complex fire destroyed 1,490 structures in 2020), earthquake risk from nearby fault lines, and tsunami exposure along the coast. Yet residents have no single, localized tool to get real-time hazard updates, find their evacuation zone, or build a disaster kit tailored to whether they live on the coast or in the mountains. During the January 2023 atmospheric river storms, cell towers went down across the Santa Cruz Mountains, leaving residents without access to online emergency info.

The Solution: StormReady CA19
A mobile-first, bilingual (English/Spanish) web app with five core modules: (A) a Zone Finder Map that instantly shows your evacuation zone using GPS, (B) a crowdsourced real-time storm/hazard reporting feed, (C) geographic disaster checklists tailored to coastal vs. mountain risks across all hazard types, (D) an offline mode that caches critical info as a downloadable PDF for when cell service fails, and (E) full Spanish language support for Watsonville and Salinas communities.

Why This Matters to Me — Arya Patel
I live in CA-19 and experienced the 2023 storms firsthand. My family didn't know which roads were closed, what evacuation zone we were in, or what supplies we actually needed for our specific area. I built this app so no family in our district has to feel that unprepared again.

4. Feature Modules (MVP)
Module A: Zone Finder Map
Uses the device's GPS to instantly display the user's Evacuation Zone ID and relevant hazard zones on an interactive map. This is the app's signature feature.
User Stories
	•	Auto-Detect Zone: When I open the app and allow location access, I immediately see my Evacuation Zone ID (e.g., SCZ-E030) overlaid on a map, with color-coded layers for flood, wildfire, tsunami, and earthquake fault zones.
	•	Search Any Address: I can type any CA-19 address to look up its evacuation zone and risk profile.
	•	Layer Toggle: I can toggle map layers on/off: Flood Zones (FEMA), Fire Hazard Severity Zones (CAL FIRE), Tsunami Inundation Zones (CGS), Earthquake Fault Lines (USGS).
	•	Zone Detail Card: When I tap my zone, I see a card with: zone ID, primary risks, nearest evacuation route, and a 'View My Checklist' button that auto-selects Coastal or Mountain.

Module B: Real-Time Community Storm Reports
A crowdsourced feed where any authenticated user can submit and view live storm/hazard reports for the CA-19 area. Reports are shown in both a chronological feed and on the Zone Finder Map.
User Stories
	•	Submit Report: I can submit a report with: hazard category, text description (max 280 chars), optional photo, and auto-detected or pin-dropped location.
	•	View Feed: I scroll through a chronological feed filtered by category or recency.
	•	Map Integration: Report pins appear on the Zone Finder Map, color-coded by category.
	•	Confirm / Flag: I can tap 'Confirm' to verify a report (boosting visibility) or 'Flag' to report misinformation.
	•	Severity Auto-Tag: Reports with 5+ confirmations in 30 minutes are auto-tagged as 'Verified — High Activity.'

Module C: Tailored Disaster Checklists
Not a generic checklist — a geographic checklist. Users select their zone (Coastal or Mountain) and the app generates a preparedness checklist covering all relevant hazards with zone-specific items.
The Twist That Makes This Special
A coastal resident in Monterey needs sandbags, high-tide schedules, and salt-spray-protected documents. A mountain resident in the Santa Cruz Mountains needs tire chains, a backup generator for PG&E outages, and knowledge of landslide warning signs (cracking soil, leaning trees). Generic apps miss all of this.

User Stories
	•	Choose Zone: I pick Coastal or Mountain and my checklist auto-populates with items specific to my risks.
	•	Multi-Hazard Sections: My checklist is organized by hazard type (earthquake, wildfire, flood, tsunami, general) so I can prepare for each threat.
	•	Check Off & Track: I tap items to mark 'Ready' and see my overall preparedness score as a percentage.
	•	Family Sharing: I can share my checklist via link so family members can see what is still needed.
	•	Info Tooltips: Each item has a small (i) icon explaining why it matters (e.g., 'Why N95 masks? Wildfire smoke contains PM2.5 particles...').

Module D: Offline Mode Protocol
Why This Is MVP, Not a Nice-to-Have
During CA-19 storms, cell towers go down in the mountains and Big Sur. An emergency app that doesn't work during emergencies is useless. Offline Mode is a core feature, not a stretch goal.

StormReady CA19's Offline Mode includes an 'Emergency PDF Generator' that creates a downloadable one-page summary accessible with zero internet.
What the Emergency PDF Contains
	•	Evacuation Zone ID: Your zone code and nearest evacuation route with turn-by-turn directions
	•	Local Emergency Numbers: Santa Cruz County OES, Monterey County OES, PG&E outage line, poison control, non-emergency police
	•	Your Checklist Status: Snapshot of your disaster kit checklist with checked/unchecked items
	•	Nearest Shelters: Pre-cached list of Red Cross shelters and community centers in your zone
	•	Family Reunion Point: User-entered meeting point address for family reunification
Technical Implementation
	•	PDF Generation: Use the browser's built-in print-to-PDF capability via a styled HTML page, or the jsPDF library for programmatic generation.
	•	Caching: Service Worker caches the last 50 reports and all checklist data. When offline, the app shows a banner: 'You are offline — showing cached data.'
	•	Prompt to Download: After a user completes their checklist, a prominent button says: 'Download Your Emergency PDF — Works Without Internet.'
	•	Storage: PDF is saved to the phone's local storage (Downloads folder); also accessible via the app's offline cache.

Module E: Bilingual Support (English / Spanish)
Why Bilingual Is Essential for CA-19
Watsonville and Salinas have populations that are over 75% Hispanic/Latino, with many households where Spanish is the primary language. A disaster app that only works in English fails the people who need it most. Bilingual support is an accessibility and equity requirement.

Implementation Plan
	•	Language Toggle: Persistent toggle in the top navigation bar: EN | ES. Selection is saved to user profile and persists across sessions.
	•	i18n Library: Use react-i18next (the most popular React internationalization library). All user-facing strings live in JSON translation files.
	•	Translation Scope: All UI text, all checklist items and descriptions, all category labels, all tooltips, zone cards, and the Emergency PDF.
	•	Community Reports: User-submitted reports stay in original language but category labels and UI chrome translate. Future: add auto-translate button per report.
	•	Translation Quality: Initial translations via AI (Claude/GPT), then reviewed by a native Spanish speaker from the community for accuracy.

6.3 Firebase Security Rules
Firestore rules ensure data safety without a custom back-end:
	•	Anyone (even logged-out users) can READ reports and checklist templates.
	•	Only authenticated users can CREATE new reports.
	•	Users can only UPDATE or DELETE their own reports (authorId must match auth UID).
	•	Users can only READ/WRITE their own checklist progress and profile.
	•	Rate limit: max 10 reports per user per hour (enforced via client-side throttle + Firestore rules).
	•	Image uploads limited to 2MB per file (enforced in Storage rules).

8. UI/UX Design Guidelines
8.1 Design Principles
	•	Emergency-first clarity: Every element must be instantly understandable during a stressful, possibly life-threatening situation.
	•	Big tap targets: All buttons at least 48x48px; category chips are large and tappable on a wet phone screen.
	•	High contrast: WCAG AA contrast ratios minimum; avoid light gray text on white.
	•	Mobile-first: 80%+ of users will be on phones. Design for 375px-wide screen first, then scale up.
	•	Progressive disclosure: Show critical info (zone ID, active alerts, top reports) first; details on tap.
	•	Bilingual by design: All UI elements must accommodate Spanish text (which is ~15% longer than English).
	•	Offline-aware: Always show connection status; degrade gracefully when offline.
